# Bill Cheat Sheet

## What tends to work

- Do the hard stuff earlier. Mornings tend to go smoother than afternoons.
- Let weather panic run its course — fighting it extends it.
- Don’t argue the point. Short, calm reassurance works better than correcting or debating.
- Sudoku or another familiar activity can interrupt a restless stretch.
- If agitation is escalating, reset by changing the environment — redirect to Sudoku or switch activities.
- Redirect instead of resisting. If he’s stuck, switch to a different task or topic.
- Move dinner earlier to get ahead of the afternoon sundowning window.
- Answer once, briefly.
- Social time with friends brings out the best — spirited and engaged.
- If he keeps looping, redirect instead of repeating.
- Validate first, then steer. “I hear you” before “let’s do this instead.”
- A short dog walk can reset his mood.
- Use one-step directions. Too many instructions at once seems to backfire.
- Switch TV away from local news and weather, especially in the afternoon.
- Keep replies calm, low-volume, and one sentence when possible.
- Golf or other familiar activities with friends work well — social and routine combined.
- Offer a choice or next step instead of a correction.

## Useful phrases

- “I hear you.”
- “That makes sense.”
- “Let’s check together.”
- “You may be right.”
- “We can come back to it.”

## What to avoid

- “I already told you”
- “That’s not true”
- Long explanations
- Arguing facts or proving it
- Correcting him in front of others

## What calm days seem to have in common


- Fewer arguments about control: calmer when nobody pushes hard on driving, timing, or plans.
- Less correction/confrontation: redirection seems to help more than debating.
- More structure, less surprise: calm days often have a familiar rhythm.
- Calm doesn’t always mean clear: he can still be confused or repetitive on a good day.

## Update as we learn

- Add things that reliably help.
- Remove or revise anything that doesn’t actually work.
- Keep this short and practical.

## When to include Bill

- Include him when the task is simple, safe, and concrete.
- Include him when you can give one step at a time and keep the ask small.
- Include him when it preserves dignity and he can do a useful part of the task.
- Do it yourself when the task is risky, time-sensitive, or physically awkward.
- Do it yourself when he starts resisting, arguing, or getting suspicious.
- If cooperation only works with a small reframing, keep it brief and face-saving.
